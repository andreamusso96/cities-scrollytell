Are we all going to live in giant cities?

One way to open this story is not with an abstract curve, but with a direct visual comparison. Put a city of 100,000 people next to one of 10 million and ask what changes when the scale changes that much. The answer is not only that one place contains more people. The larger city becomes a different kind of machine. It can sustain more occupations, more specialized firms, deeper supplier networks, and a denser exchange of ideas.

That does not mean giant cities are automatically better. It means they amplify things. The same scale that makes a city more productive can also make it more expensive, more pressurized, and harder to inhabit. So the first scene should make that amplification intuitive before the article moves on to the measurement problem.

If the opening works, the reader now feels the stakes without yet needing a line chart.

The next move in the article is to ask whether the world is really moving toward that giant-city future. That is where the measurement problem begins. To compare urban growth honestly, we first need a stable idea of what a city is, how far it extends, and how to follow the same city across decades while its physical footprint changes.

That is the bridge into the data section. The opening gives the reader a reason to care; the methodology then earns the authority to answer the larger question.
