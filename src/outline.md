Are then Asia and Africa are headed in a future of megacities. Not quite! They are just earlier in the same lifecycle. 

South Korea is a prime example. Look at South Korea. In the 1970s and 80s, Seoul grew at a dizzying pace. But as the country matured, Seoul's special advantage vanished.

VIZ a line chart showing Seoul's population pulling away from "Rest of Korea" and then leveling off

This is seen also in the curves of growth vs size 

VIZ South Korea size growth curve slowly moving down acros years and becoming flat. 

The same thing happened in the United States, but much earlier. A big-city advantage was strong in the 19th century, but faded as the nation urbanized.

VIZ US size growth curve slowly moving down acros years and becoming flat. 

This is the **urban lifecycle**.

Nations go through an "urban adolescence"—a chaotic growth spurt where megacities are forged. But they eventually enter "urban adulthood," where growth becomes stable and balanced.

VIZ: The idealized animation of a flattening size-growth curve, labeled "Adolescence -> Adulthood.”

But the adolescent growth spurt leaves behind a permanent legacy: **a world of giants.**

These dominant cities, forged in the past, continue to act as our primary amplifiers. Their locked-in dominance means they will continue to supercharge our best ideas and our worst problems. The lifecycle doesn't erase the giants; it just explains how they were made.
